About the Trust

A quiet trust for a noisy century.

We do one thing: we look after woods. Five of them, each a Hundred Acres, scattered across Europe and held in perpetuity for the wildlife that lives there and the people who walk through them.

Honeypot Quarters was founded in the autumn of 1926 by a small circle of foresters, librarians and a single, anonymous benefactor who insisted only that the woods be kept "as they are, or better." That instruction has guided every decision since.

We are a private trust, not a tourist company. Our properties are open to the public, freely, but we do not market them. There is no gift shop. There is no café (except in Sussex, on Sundays, and only for tea). What there is, in great quantity, is woodland — managed lightly, surveyed annually, and shared willingly.

The Hundred Acre Woods are not five copies of one place. The Sussex wood is hornbeam and oak; the Dordogne, chestnut; Andalusia, cork and pine; Tuscany, holm and umbrella; Krasnodar, beech and silver fir. What unites them is a single covenant — that no acre be enclosed, drained, felled for sale, or improved beyond recognition.

Heritage

A hundred years, kept lightly.

The trust has changed hands four times and its premises only twice. The covenant has not changed at all.

1926

The trust is founded

Established in Hartfield, Sussex, by a small consortium of foresters and a single anonymous benefactor. The founding charter, two pages long, is still kept at the Sussex warden's lodge.

1948

Le Bois de Cent Acres acquired

Purchased from the Périgord commune after the war, on the condition that the limestone pilgrim path remain open to walkers without charge — a condition the trust has honoured for seventy-eight years.

1972

The Andalusia covenant

El Bosque de las Cien Acres is acquired from a cooperative of cork farmers. The cork harvest continues, by hand, on the original nine-year cycle.

1989

Tuscany joins the trust

Il Bosco dei Cento Acri is donated by the estate of a former trustee. The 14th-century chapel ruin within the wood is preserved, by deliberate policy, as ruin.

2004

The Caucasus wood is added

Сто-Акровый Лес in Krasnodar Krai becomes the fifth and final wood. Its acquisition takes eleven years of negotiation and remains the trust's largest single project.

2026

The Centenary

The trust marks one hundred years with the publication of A Hundred Acres, A Hundred Years — a single-volume survey of the five woods, available at each warden's lodge.

The Code of the Wood

Five principles, kept since 1926.

The founding charter is short. These are its provisions, rendered into plain English, and printed on the back of every visitor's map.

Leave it as you found it

Or, if you must change something, change it for the better. No fences raised that need not be raised; no path widened that need not be widened.

The wood is not a product

No timber is sold. No acre is enclosed. No commercial use is made of the land beyond the cork harvest in Andalusia and the Sussex Sunday teas.

Visitors are guests

The woods are free to walk, all year, in daylight. Wardens are present to help, not to charge. Dogs welcome on leads; bicycles on marked tracks only.

Wildlife comes first

If a path disturbs a sett, the path is moved. If a tree falls across a track, it is sawn through and left in place. The wood is for those who live in it.

Survey every year

Each wood is walked end-to-end every autumn by its warden and a single trustee. The survey is one page long. It has been filed, without interruption, since 1927.

Trustees & Wardens

A small board, in person, in autumn.

The trust is governed by five trustees and five resident wardens — one of each per wood. The full board meets once a year, on the first Saturday of November, at the Sussex lodge.

Visiting

You are already welcome.

There is no admission charge, no booking, and no map to download — maps are kept at each warden's lodge and are yours to take. Please go gently, follow the marked paths, and leave the wood as you found it. Or, if you must, better.

For correspondence — including the annual report, warden contacts, or the centenary volume — write to us directly.
