I.The Trust

Honeypot Quarters Limited was founded in the autumn of 1926 by a small consortium of Sussex foresters, a county librarian, and a single anonymous benefactor whose only instruction was that the woods be kept “as they are, or better.” That instruction has guided every decision of the trust since.

The trust holds five woodlands, each of one hundred acres, in five countries. It does not own anything else. It does not, by its constitution, do anything else. It is governed by five trustees and tended by five resident wardens, and meets in person, once a year, at Hartfield in Sussex, on the first Saturday of November.

There is no commercial activity, save the Andalusian cork harvest and the Sussex Sunday teas.1 The woods are open. There is no charge. The trust has, for one hundred years, considered this sufficient.

  1. The teas, served at the warden’s lodge from three to five o’clock between Easter and Michaelmas, are provided at cost and are not, strictly, a commercial activity. The trust raises no objection to the distinction. ↩

II.The Five Woods

  1. The Hundred Acre Wood

    Sussex, England · The Original

    The wood from which the trust takes its name, and the only wood named in the founding charter. Hornbeam and English oak across the lower slopes, bracken on the open ground, and a bee tree of considerable age standing near the western boundary. The rope bridges spanning the dell are replaced each spring by the warden, by hand.2

    Founded 1926Acreage 100Warden Mr R. Hartfield (the elder)Open daily, dawn to dusk

  2. Le Bois de Cent Acres

    Dordogne, France · Acquired 1948

    Sweet chestnut and walnut on a limestone shelf above the river. A monastic pilgrim path crosses the wood from east to west and has been kept open, unbroken, since the twelfth century — a condition of the trust’s purchase from the Périgord commune. A single beehive, maintained on behalf of the commune since 1951, stands at the path’s northern bend.

    Acquired 1948Acreage 100Warden Mme C. LebrunOpen daily, dawn to dusk

  3. El Bosque de las Cien Acres

    Andalusia, Spain · The Cork Wood · Acquired 1972

    Cork oak and umbrella pine on warm Andalucían hillside in the Sierra de Aracena. The cork is harvested by hand every ninth year, on the cycle established before the trust’s purchase, and returned to local cooperatives. An Iberian lynx has been recorded in the wood on three separate occasions, the most recent in 2023.

    Acquired 1972Acreage 100Warden Sr. M. Vega CarrascoOpen daily, dawn to dusk

  4. Il Bosco dei Cento Acri

    Tuscany, Italy · The Stone Wood · Acquired 1989

    Holm oak and umbrella pine in the Val d’Orcia, and at the wood’s centre the ruin of a fourteenth-century chapel that is, by deliberate policy of the trust, kept as ruin.3 An old pilgrim path crosses the property, marked by no signs and maintained by no one in particular.

    Acquired 1989Acreage 100Warden Sig. T. Bellini (also trustee)Open daily, dawn to dusk

  5. Сто-Акровый Лес

    Krasnodar Krai, Russia · The Western Caucasus · Acquired 2004

    Caucasian beech and silver fir on the northern slopes; the oldest of the five woods by tree-age, with specimens recorded at more than four hundred years. The trust’s longest acquisition: eleven years of negotiation, first letter to final deed. Closed during the winter months on the warden’s recommendation.

    Acquired 2004Acreage 100Warden Mr P. VolkovOpen May–October, daylight hours

  1. The original bridges, of 1927, were replaced annually until 1971, at which point a sturdier pattern was adopted; this pattern, with minor refinements, is the one in use today. ↩
  2. A proposal in 1994 to restore the chapel was unanimously rejected. The trust’s position, recorded in the minutes, was that “a ruin is a thing in its own right.” ↩

III.The Code of the Wood

The founding charter is short. These are its five provisions, in plain English, and printed on the back of every visitor’s map.

  1. Leave it as you found it. If you must change something, change it for the better.

  2. The wood is not a product. No timber shall be sold; no acre shall be enclosed; no commercial use shall be made of the land, save the Andalusian cork harvest and the Sussex Sunday teas.

  3. Visitors are guests. The woods shall be open, freely, in daylight, in all seasons in which they are open. Wardens are present to assist, not to charge.

  4. Wildlife comes first. If a path disturbs a sett, the path is moved. If a tree falls across a track, it is sawn through and left in place.

  5. Survey every year. Each wood shall be walked end-to-end every autumn by its warden and a trustee, and the survey filed at the Sussex lodge. The survey is to be one page long, and not longer.

IV.Notes for the Season

May MMXXVI

It is May. In Sussex, the bluebells are over and the bracken is up. In the Dordogne, the chestnuts are in leaf and the bees are at the lime. The cork in Andalusia is one year from its next harvest. The chapel at Tuscany is, as ever, ruined. Krasnodar has been open for the season since the second of the month.

V.A Hundred Years, in Short

  1. 1926

    The trust is founded at Hartfield, Sussex.

  2. 1927

    The first annual survey is filed (reproduced at §VIII).

  3. 1939–45

    Surveys continue, on shortened forms, throughout the war. The wartime forms are kept separately at the lodge.

  4. 1948

    Le Bois de Cent Acres is purchased from the Périgord commune.

  5. 1971

    The Sussex rope bridges are redesigned, for the first and last time.

  6. 1972

    El Bosque de las Cien Acres is acquired from a cork-farming cooperative.

  7. 1989

    Il Bosco dei Cento Acri is bequeathed by the estate of a former trustee.

  8. 1994

    A proposal to restore the Tuscany chapel is unanimously rejected.

  9. 2004

    Сто-Акровый Лес joins the trust, after eleven years of negotiation.

  10. 2023

    An Iberian lynx is recorded in Andalusia for the third time.

  11. 2026

    The trust marks its centenary. This notice is published.

VII.On Visiting

There is no admission charge, no booking, and no map to download. Maps, where they exist, are kept at each warden’s lodge and are yours to take. There are no signs at the gates beyond the wood’s name. Dogs welcome, on leads, in all five woods; bicycles on marked tracks only, in Sussex and Krasnodar.

Please go gently, follow the paths where they exist, and leave the wood as you found it. Or, if you must, better.
