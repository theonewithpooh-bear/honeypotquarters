The Founding Charter

Two pages, five articles. Set down by the founders at Hartfield on the day the trust was registered.

“We, the undersigned, being foresters and friends to woods, do this day establish a trust for the keeping of woodland, in perpetuity, against all uses save the use of being a wood.”

I.

Leave it as you found it. If you must change something, change it for the better.

II.

The wood is not a product. No timber shall be sold; no acre shall be enclosed; no commercial use shall be made of the land, save the Andalusian cork harvest and the Sussex Sunday teas.

III.

Visitors are guests. The woods shall be open, freely, in daylight, in all seasons in which they are open. Wardens are present to assist, not to charge.

IV.

Wildlife comes first. If a path disturbs a sett, the path is moved. If a tree falls across a track, it is sawn through and left in place.

V.

Survey every year. Each wood shall be walked end-to-end every autumn by its warden and a trustee, and the survey filed at the Sussex lodge. The survey is to be one page long, and not longer.

Set down this fourteenth day of October, in the year nineteen hundred and twenty-six,
at the lodge in Hartfield, in the county of East Sussex.

R. Hartfield · A. Carlile · the friend to woods

Sealed in beeswax on the same day. The seal is reproduced below.

The trust’s seal — honeycomb with a single bee, struck in beeswax. Pressed onto every original document since 1926, and at the foot of each year’s annual survey.

The charter has not been amended. A proposal in 1994 to amend Article II in order to permit the restoration of the Tuscany chapel was unanimously rejected; no amendment has been proposed since.
