Wood IV.

Il Bosco dei Cento Acri

Tuscany, Italy · The Stone Wood

Holm oak and umbrella pine in the Val d’Orcia, and at the wood’s centre the ruin of a fourteenth-century chapel that is, by deliberate policy of the trust, kept as ruin. An old pilgrim path crosses the property from north to south, marked by no signs and maintained by no one in particular.

The wood was bequeathed to the trust in 1989 by the estate of Tommaso Bellini the elder, a founding-era trustee. The current warden, Tommaso Bellini the younger, is his grandson, and the only person to serve simultaneously as warden and trustee in the trust’s hundred years.

A proposal in 1994 to restore the chapel was unanimously rejected. The minutes record the board’s position as: “a ruin is a thing in its own right.” No proposal has been made since.

“Una rovina è una cosa in sé.”

— the board’s position, 1994; also engraved on the Bellini stone

The wood is reached on foot from the village by the old pilgrim path itself, or from the western lane by a short cart-track. There is no warden’s lodge; the warden lives in the village, twenty minutes’ walk away, and is generally to be found at the wood between the hours of nine and noon.

The chapel
Ruined by policy, since 1994

The chapel is the only thing inside any of the trust’s woods that the trust does not own. Its title rests with the Diocese of Pienza, which has not visited since 1971.

In this wood

  • The West Gate — opens onto the old cart lane, lined with cypresses.
  • The Pilgrim Path — north–south, passes within thirty yards of the chapel.
  • The Chapel — 14th-century; ruined by policy since 1994.
  • The Old Cistern — Roman, dry since the seventeenth century.
  • The Holm Oak Stand — the wood’s central grove.
  • The Pine Edge — umbrella pines along the eastern boundary.
  • The Bellini Stone — a memorial bench placed by the donor’s family in 1991.
