Wood III.

El Bosque de las Cien Acres

Andalusia, Spain · The Cork Wood

Cork oak and umbrella pine on warm Andalucían hillside in the Sierra de Aracena. The cork is harvested every ninth year, by hand, on the cycle established long before the trust’s 1972 purchase, and returned in full to the local cooperative without charge.

The harvest is the only commercial activity at any of the trust’s five woods. The trust takes no share of the proceeds; the cork is the cooperative’s, and has been since at least 1810. The next harvest is in 2027.

The Iberian lynx is not resident in the wood but has been recorded on three separate occasions, in 1981, 2009, and 2023. The warden, when asked whether the lynx will return, refers the question to the lynx.

“The wood is, by trust policy, kept as it would be without us. The cork comes off because it has always come off. Nothing else does.”

— Sr. M. Vega Carrasco, warden

Visitors are asked to keep to the marked tracks during the harvest months (October and November of the harvest year). Outside the cycle, all paths are open. Water is scarce on the property between July and September; bring your own.

In this wood

  • The South Gate — by the old shepherds’ path from the village.
  • The Cork Grove — the wood’s primary stand, harvested every ninth year.
  • The Harvest Track — the cart road used in October of the harvest year.
  • The Pine Crest — umbrella pines along the upper ridge.
  • The Lynx Stones — three boulders where a lynx has been recorded.
  • The Arroyo — runs north–south; dry from July to September.
  • The Cork Hut — locked outside the harvest cycle.
