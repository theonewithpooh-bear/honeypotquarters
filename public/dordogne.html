Wood II.

Le Bois de Cent Acres

Dordogne, France · Acquired 1948

Sweet chestnut and walnut on a limestone shelf above the river. A monastic pilgrim path crosses the wood from east to west, kept unbroken since the twelfth century — a condition of the trust’s purchase from the Périgord commune in 1948.

The wood was the trust’s first acquisition outside England. Negotiations were brief: the commune wanted only that the path remain open to walkers, without charge, and the trust wanted only that the limestone shelf remain unfarmed. Both terms have been kept for seventy-eight years.

A single beehive, maintained on the commune’s behalf since 1951, stands at the path’s northern bend. The hive is one of three things at any of the trust’s woods not owned by the trust — the others being a 14th-century chapel in Tuscany and a small wartime memorial at the Caucasus property.

“Le sentier reste ouvert. C’est tout.”

— the only written provision in the 1948 sale, from the Périgord commune

The wood is reached on foot from the village of Sarlat by the old pilgrim path itself, a walk of forty minutes. There is no car park. The warden’s cottage stands half a mile north, outside the wood’s boundary, on land not held by the trust.

Surveyed autumn 2024 · One page only

In this wood

  • The Northern Gate — narrow, marked by an old limestone post.
  • The Chestnut Grove — old sweet chestnut, the wood’s largest stand.
  • The Pilgrim Path — limestone, east–west, since the twelfth century.
  • The Beehive — single hive, the commune’s, since 1951.
  • The Old Stone Wall — partly collapsed, kept that way by the warden.
  • The Walnut Stand — younger trees on the south side, planted 1962.
  • The River — the southern boundary; deep enough to swim at the eastern bend.
