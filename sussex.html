Wood I.

The Hundred Acre Wood

Sussex, England · The Original

The wood from which the trust takes its name. Hornbeam and English oak across the lower slopes; bracken on the open ground; bluebells, in the third week of April, for about nine days. The dell at the centre is crossed by three rope bridges, replaced each spring by the warden, by hand.

Of the trust’s five woods, Sussex is the only one continuously held since the trust’s founding. The wood was acquired in October 1926 from the family of the founding benefactor, on the same day the trust was registered at Companies House.

The warden’s lodge stands at the south boundary, by the lower gate, and is the only building owned by the trust at any of its properties. It is where the November board meeting is held, where the Sunday teas are served between Easter and Michaelmas, and where the 1926 charter and all subsequent survey forms are kept — in a single cabinet, save for the wartime forms of 1939 to 1945, which are kept separately.

“Roe deer; tawny owl; one bear (seen near the bee tree).”

— wildlife entry, the 1927 annual survey

The wood is open every day of the year, from dawn to dusk. Dogs are welcome on leads. Bicycles are permitted on the upper path between the dell and the northern boundary, and on no other path. There are no signs at the gates beyond the wood’s name.

A plan of the wood

As drawn by the warden, autumn 2024

Seven features are marked. The map is redrawn each year following the November survey, and a copy is kept at the lodge for visitors.

Surveyed autumn 2024 · One page only

Marked features

  1. 1The Warden’s Lodge — south gate; the only building at the wood.
  2. 2The Lower Path — from the gate to the dell, about 200 yards.
  3. 3The Dell — three rope bridges, replaced each spring by hand.
  4. 4The Bee Tree — a hornbeam of considerable age, with a long-resident colony.
  5. 5The Upper Path — from the dell to the northern stump; bicycles permitted.
  6. 6The North Pole (so-called) — a hawthorn stump on the northern boundary.
  7. 7Eeyore’s Field — a small east-facing meadow, kept by no one in particular.
